Dajjal

"He who shall deceive the world at the End of Times"

☽ Islam — Al-Masīḥ ad-Dajjāl ✝ Christianity — Antichrist ✡ Judaism — Armilus

Origin & Nature

Across the three Abrahamic faiths, a terrifying figure looms at the edge of time — a being of immense power, magnificent deception, and one blinded eye. In Islam, he is Al-Masīḥ ad-Dajjāl, "the Deceiving Messiah," a man of extraordinary ability who will claim divinity and lead multitudes astray.

In Christian eschatology, a parallel figure emerges — the Antichrist — described in the letters of John and elaborated in Revelation as the Beast, the man of lawlessness who sets himself above God. In Jewish tradition, the figure of Armilus in later midrashic literature bears startling similarities: a false king who defeats early attempts at redemption before being destroyed by the true Meshiah.

"Between the creation of Adam and the coming of the Hour, there is no matter greater than the Dajjal." — Hadith, Sahih Muslim

His distinguishing mark across traditions: he is blind or defective in one eye, and the word Kafir (disbeliever) is written on his forehead — visible to every true believer. He commands fire and rivers, resurrects the dead as an illusion, and travels the earth at tremendous speed.

THE DECEIVER — Al-Masīḥ ad-Dajjāl

Imprisonment & Release

Islamic tradition holds that the Dajjal is currently bound on an island, chained, awaiting his appointed time. He is visited by Tamim ad-Dari, a companion of the Prophet ﷺ, who described encountering a giant creature in chains on a mysterious island who asked about the state of the world and whether the final Prophet had come.

In the Book of Revelation, a parallel binding is described — Satan is chained for a thousand years before being released "for a little while." The convergence of these traditions suggests a shared eschatological memory: the great deceiver is not destroyed but contained, patiently waiting for his hour.

"He is chained on an island... his left eye is blind, his right eye protrudes like a floating grape." — Hadith, Sahih Muslim 2942

Christian theologians throughout history have debated the identity of the Antichrist — Nero, the Pope, Napoleon, various modern leaders — reflecting the deep anxiety this figure generates in each age.

The Birth of a Miracle

In all three Abrahamic traditions, the figure of Jesus — Isa in Arabic, Yeshua in Hebrew — occupies a unique and contested space. For Muslims, he is one of the greatest prophets, born miraculously of the Virgin Maryam, given the miracle of speech as an infant and the ability to heal the blind and lepers. For Christians, he is the Son of God incarnate, the second person of the Trinity, whose death and resurrection opened the path of salvation.

In Judaism, the historical Yeshua is acknowledged but not recognized as Messiah; his story is viewed through a different lens — a Jewish rabbi whose movement evolved into a separate religion. Yet even in Jewish mystical texts, there are references to a "anointed one" whose life and sacrifice remain a theological puzzle.

"O Maryam! Verily Allah gives you glad tidings of a Word from Him, whose name will be the Messiah Jesus, son of Maryam, held in honor in this world and in the Hereafter." — Quran 3:45

The virgin birth, the speaking infant, the miracles — all traditions agree on the extraordinary nature of his entry into the world, though they interpret its meaning differently.

The Anointed One — Three Interpretations

The word "Messiah" — Mashiach in Hebrew, Masih in Arabic, Christ in Greek — means "the anointed one." It describes a figure who is consecrated with oil for a divine mission: prophet, priest, or king. The great theological divide of the Abrahamic faiths centers on who this figure is and whether they have come.

For Judaism, the Meshiah is still to come — a human descendant of King David who will restore the Jewish kingdom, rebuild the Temple in Jerusalem, gather the dispersed Jewish people, and usher in an era of universal peace and knowledge of God. No supernatural attributes are required; he is a great human leader.

"The Meshiah will be a great political leader, descended from King David. He will be well versed in Jewish law... and will observe its commandments. He will be a charismatic leader, inspiring others to follow his example." — Maimonides, Mishneh Torah

In Christianity, the Meshiah has already come in the person of Jesus Christ — with a spiritual kingdom rather than political. In Islam, the Messiah is Isa ibn Maryam who shall return in the end times to establish justice upon the earth.

The Creator

"In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God"

The Infinite Divine

At the heart of all three Abrahamic faiths lies a singular conviction: there is one God, the Creator of all existence, before whom no other power stands. Yet the three traditions articulate this unity in profoundly different ways, leading to distinct theologies, worship practices, and visions of the human-divine relationship.

In Islam, God is Allah — The God — possessing 99 Beautiful Names (Al-Asma al-Husna) that reveal His attributes: Al-Rahman (The Most Merciful), Al-Khaliq (The Creator), Al-Hakim (The All-Wise). He is absolutely One (Tawhid), transcendent beyond all description, yet "closer to you than your jugular vein."

"Say: He is Allah, the One! Allah, the Eternal, Absolute. He begets not, nor was He begotten. And there is none comparable unto Him." — Quran 112:1-4 (Al-Ikhlas)

In Judaism, God is YHWH (the unpronounceable Tetragrammaton) — Adonai in prayer. Jewish mysticism describes Ein Sof ("without end"), the infinite Divine beyond all attributes. The Shema prayer encapsulates all: "Hear O Israel: the Lord our God, the Lord is One."

Christianity upholds the mystery of the Trinity: the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit — three persons, one Being. This doctrine, refined at the Council of Nicaea (325 CE), distinguishes Christianity most sharply from Islam and Judaism.

The Creation
"Be!" — The Word of Creation
In Islam, the Creator's method of creation is the divine command "Kun!" (Be!). The Quran states: "When He decrees a matter, He only says 'Be!' and it is." This same creative word — the Logos — appears in John 1:1 and parallels the Hebrew "Yehi" (Let there be) of Genesis.

02
Cosmogony
Creation in Six Days
Genesis describes creation over six days: light, firmament, land and sea, celestial bodies, creatures, and finally Adam. The Quran confirms creation in six "periods" (ayyam), leaving open whether these are literal or cosmic epochs. On the seventh day, the Bible says God rested; the Quran states God does not tire.
☽ Islam✝ Christian✡ Judaism
03
Creation of Humanity
Adam Created from Clay and Divine Breath
God fashioned Adam from clay (or dust) and breathed His own spirit (ruh/neshamah) into him. This act makes humans unique — carrying within them a divine spark. The angels were commanded to bow to Adam; Iblis (Satan) refused out of pride, beginning the cosmic drama of free will and disobedience.
☽ Islam✝ Christian✡ Judaism
04
Christian Theology
The Mystery of the Trinity
Christianity proclaims one God existing as three coequal, coeternal persons: the Father (Creator), the Son (Redeemer), and the Holy Spirit (Sanctifier). The doctrine developed over centuries through councils, creeds, and theological debate. It remains the central theological distinction from Islam and Judaism.
✝ Christian
05
Jewish Theology
The Ineffable Name — YHWH
The four Hebrew letters Yod-Heh-Vav-Heh (YHWH) form the most sacred name of God in Judaism, revealed to Moses at the burning bush. It is so holy that it is never pronounced — substituted with "Adonai" (Lord) in prayer and "HaShem" (The Name) in daily speech. Its original pronunciation is lost.
✡ Judaism
06
Islamic Theology
The 99 Beautiful Names of Allah
The Quran and Sunnah describe Allah through 99 names — Al-Asma al-Husna — each revealing a divine attribute: Al-Rahman (The Merciful), Al-Ghafur (The Forgiving), Al-Qahhar (The Subduer), Al-Wadud (The Loving). The Prophet ﷺ said: "Allah has 99 names; whoever memorizes them enters Paradise."
☽ Islam
07
Divine Revelation
God Speaks to Moses at the Burning Bush
At Mount Horeb, God appeared to Moses in a burning bush that was not consumed by flame. He revealed His name as "I AM WHO I AM" (Ehyeh Asher Ehyeh), commissioned Moses to free the Israelites from Egypt, and began the most documented human-divine dialogue in scripture.
✡ Judaism✝ Christian☽ Islam
08
Proclamation of Oneness
The Shema — "Hear O Israel: God is One"
The Shema (Deuteronomy 6:4) is the central declaration of Jewish faith and the prayer said at morning, evening, and on one's deathbed: "Shema Yisrael: Adonai Eloheinu, Adonai Echad." It mirrors the Islamic Shahada's declaration of Tawhid and is the theological foundation of all Abrahamic monotheism.
✡ Judaism☽ Islam
09
Divine Law
The Ten Commandments at Mount Sinai
God descended upon Mount Sinai in fire and thunder, the mountain trembling, and gave Moses the Ten Commandments written on stone tablets by the "finger of God." These commandments form the ethical foundation of all three Abrahamic faiths and much of Western law and morality.
✡ Judaism✝ Christian☽ Islam
10
Final Revelation
The Quran Revealed Over 23 Years
Allah revealed the Quran to the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ through the angel Jibreel over 23 years — beginning in the Cave of Hira in 610 CE. The Quran is considered the literal word of God in Arabic, preserved without alteration. Muslims believe it is the final divine message to humanity.
☽ Islam
11
Divine Judgment
The Great Flood and Noah's Ark
God's grief at human corruption led to the Great Flood — a divine reset of creation. Noah (Nuh in Islam) was commanded to build an ark and preserve life. The flood is recorded in Genesis, the Quran (Surah Nuh), and ancient Mesopotamian texts, suggesting a universal cultural memory of this event.
☽ Islam✝ Christian✡ Judaism
12
Jewish Mysticism
Ein Sof — The Infinite Without End
Kabbalistic Judaism describes the ultimate divine reality as Ein Sof — "without end." Beyond all names and attributes. Creation flows through ten Sefirot (divine emanations), channeling infinite light into the finite world. God is simultaneously utterly transcendent and intimately present in every atom of existence.
✡ Judaism
13
Divine Test
God Tests Ibrahim / Abraham
God commanded Ibrahim to sacrifice his beloved son. At the moment of sacrifice God intervened, providing a ram. This supreme test established the Abrahamic covenant. It is commemorated annually in Islam as Eid al-Adha, and echoed in the Jewish reading of the Akedah on Rosh Hashanah.
☽ Islam✝ Christian✡ Judaism
14
First Act
"Let There Be Light" — The First Creation
The first divine command in Genesis is "Yehi Or" — Let there be light. This primordial light preceded the sun and stars by three days, leading Kabbalists to call it Or Ein Sof — the infinite spiritual light still hidden within creation, accessible only to the righteous in the World to Come.
✡ Judaism✝ Christian☽ Islam
15
Divine Mercy
Ar-Rahman — The Overflowing Mercy
A hadith states God has divided mercy into 100 parts, keeping 99 for the Day of Judgment and releasing only one part onto all of creation — and from that single drop of mercy, every mother's tenderness, every creature's compassion flows. The fullness of divine mercy awaits those who return to Him.
☽ Islam✝ Christian✡ Judaism
16
Supreme Verse
Ayat al-Kursi — The Throne Verse
Quran 2:255 is considered the mightiest verse in existence: "Allah — there is no deity except Him, the Ever-Living, the Sustainer. Neither drowsiness overtakes Him nor sleep. His Kursi (throne) extends over the heavens and earth, and their preservation tires Him not." Recited for protection by Muslims worldwide.
☽ Islam
17
Christian Doctrine
The Incarnation — God Becomes Human
Christianity's most radical claim: the infinite God became finite flesh in Jesus Christ. Two natures — fully divine, fully human — in one person. Rejected by Islam (which upholds God's absolute transcendence) and Judaism, but for Christians, this act of divine humility is the supreme expression of God's love.
✝ Christian
18
Final Reckoning
The Day of Judgment — Yawm al-Qiyamah
All three faiths affirm a final Day when God resurrects every soul and judges their deeds with perfect justice. In Islam: the scales will be set, deeds weighed, and every soul will cross the Sirat bridge. In Judaism: God as judge sits on the throne of justice. In Christianity: Christ returns as judge of all.
☽ Islam✝ Christian✡ Judaism
19
Divine Presence
The Shekinah — God Dwells Among Us
In Jewish mysticism, the Shekinah is the feminine divine presence that fills sacred spaces — first the Tabernacle, then the Temple. After the Temple's destruction, the Shekinah went into exile with the Jewish people. The Talmud teaches it rests between husband and wife in a loving marriage.
✡ Judaism✝ Christian
20
Covenant
God's Eternal Covenant with Humanity
From Noah to Abraham to Moses to Muhammad ﷺ, God has repeatedly initiated covenants with humanity — promises, obligations, and relationships. The Abrahamic Covenant, the Sinai Covenant, the New Covenant of Christianity, and the Final Message of Islam are all chapters of one divine story of relationship.
☽ Islam✝ Christian✡ Judaism
21
Ultimate Destiny
Olam Ha-Ba — The World to Come
Beyond judgment and resurrection, all three traditions envision an eternal state of divine proximity. In Islam: Jannah (Paradise), where "no eye has seen, no ear has heard, and no heart has imagined." In Judaism: Olam Ha-Ba, basking in the divine light. In Christianity: the New Jerusalem, dwelling with God forever.
